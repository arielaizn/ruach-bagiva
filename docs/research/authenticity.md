# Cultural Authenticity Research: Hilltop Youth & Shepherding Outposts (Samaria Hills)

Purpose: descriptive, neutral reference for the game's art, writing, audio, naming, and event design.
Scope: lifestyle texture only. No political framing. All in-game text will be Hebrew; this doc is English
with Hebrew terms transliterated + given in Hebrew script where useful for the writers.

Sources consulted: Hebrew Wikipedia (נוער הגבעות, החוות החקלאיות ביהודה ושומרון, חוות בודדים),
Makor Rishon, Mako/N12 features, HaShomer Yosh volunteer pages, Israel Nature & Parks Authority
(wildlife-damage pages), Times of Israel outpost features, academic anthropological work
(Friedman et al., "Hilltop youth: political-anthropological research"), plus general cultural knowledge.
Estimates ~2021: roughly 400 youth spread across ~80 hills/farms; today 100+ "shepherd farms"
(חוות רועים) across Judea & Samaria, most run by one family plus a handful of youth/volunteers.

## 1. The Physical Outpost: Structures

An outpost (מאחז) or farm (חווה) is small, improvised, and grows organically. Nothing looks
factory-new; everything is patched, dusty, second-hand, and functional. Typical building inventory:

- **Caravan (קרוואן)** — the classic. Old white/beige mobile home, rust streaks, plywood porch bolted
  on, water boiler (dud shemesh) strapped to the roof, Israeli flag on a bent pole. Often towed up at
  night. Interior: bunk beds, a bookshelf of sefarim (religious books), gas burner.
- **Shipping container (מכולה)** — painted or bare, used as storage, feed shed, or converted housing
  with a window cut with an angle grinder. Doors chalk-marked in Hebrew.
- **Wooden shack / cabin (צריף)** — self-built from pallets, plywood, corrugated tin roof weighted with
  rocks and old tires. Beloved because it can be rebuilt in a day. Some youth live in tents or under
  the stars during rebuilds.
- **Sheep/goat pen (דיר / מכלאה)** — welded pipe fencing or wooden pallets wired together, shade net
  (רשת צל) stretched on top, feeding troughs from cut blue plastic barrels, a hay pile under tarp.
- **Pergola (פרגולה)** — pallet-wood posts, shade net or palm fronds; the outdoor "dining room" with a
  plastic table, mismatched plastic chairs, and a netilat-yadayim washing cup on a nail.
- **Zula (זולה)** — the chill corner: old sofas rescued from town, rugs on dirt, a low table, guitar
  leaning on a pole, string of dead fairy lights, hookah, tea kettle blackened by fire. Sacred to morale.
- **Water tower / tank (מגדל מים)** — black or white plastic tank (1–5 m³) on a welded steel stand or on
  the high point of the hill; gravity-fed hoses snake to the pen and kitchen. Refilled by tractor-hauled
  tank or jerrycans from a spring.
- **Generator (גנרטור)** — diesel, loud, temperamental. Runs a few hours in the evening; its silence at
  night is when you hear the jackals. Increasingly paired with a couple of dusty solar panels + truck
  batteries.
- **Campfire circle (מדורה)** — stone ring, blackened kumkum (kettle), sooty poyke pot, wooden benches
  or stumps. Center of night life.
- **Outhouse / field shower** — compost toilet shack, shower bag or cold hose behind tin sheeting.
- **Synagogue corner** — a caravan or shack with an ark (sometimes just a curtained cabinet), stacked
  plastic chairs, a shelf of siddurim; on bigger farms a proper small beit knesset.
- **Chicken coop, dog posts, hay barn, tool conex** — as the farm matures.
- **Access road** — bulldozed dirt track, marked by whitewashed stones, a chained gate of welded pipes.

Visual grammar for artists: white/tan caravans, silver-grey tin, raw pallet wood, black water tanks,
blue barrels, green shade-net, rust, rock piles, flags. Everything low, scattered, non-orthogonal.

## 2. Agriculture & Herding

- **The flock is the core.** Sheep (mostly Assaf/Awassi cross look: white body, sometimes brown head)
  and goats (black/brown, floppy ears). Flocks of 30–300. Herding is also how land is worked: the flock
  goes out morning and afternoon to graze the hillsides, guided by a shepherd (often a 15–19-year-old)
  with a stick, a dog, sometimes a donkey, sometimes a sling (קלע) swung to crack and steer the flock.
- **Named animals.** Shepherds genuinely name lead ewes, goats, dogs and donkeys. The lead goat wears
  a bell. Bottle-fed orphan lambs become pets that follow people around.
- **Milking (חליבה)** — by hand or a small vacuum machine, dawn and dusk in season. Milk goes to
  cheese/yogurt making (goat cheese in brine, labneh) sold to nearby communities or at junctions.
- **Vineyard (כרם)** — young vines on trellis wires, drip lines, harvest (בציר) in Elul/late summer;
  some farms press their own wine.
- **Olive grove (זית)** — planted rows plus ancient gnarled trees on old terraces; harvest (מסיק) in
  autumn with tarps under trees, hand rakes, sacks to the press; fresh oil is a point of pride.
- **Other crops:** fig trees, pomegranates, almonds, a vegetable patch (tomatoes, cucumbers, zucchini),
  wheat/barley plot for hay, herbs (zaatar, sage/marva for tea).
- **Seasonal cycle:** winter = lambing season, mud, green hills, planting; spring = shearing (גז),
  peak grazing, cheese season; summer = drought, hauling water, guarding ripening grapes from boars;
  autumn = olive harvest, plowing, first rain (יורה) celebrated like a holiday.
- **Donkey logistics (חמור)** — hauling jerrycans, feed sacks and pallet wood up trails no vehicle
  manages; the farm truck is typically a battered white pickup or an old tractor.
- **Water:** a nearby spring (עין / מעיין), sometimes cleaned out and pooled for dipping; jerrycans
  (ג'ריקנים) everywhere; rain cisterns (בורות מים) from ancient times still collect winter rain.

## 3. Daily & Weekly Routine

A believable weekday (loosely):

- **05:30** — wake before sun; netilat yadayim; shacharit prayer (some with tefillin at a plastic table
  facing Jerusalem, i.e., south from Samaria); strong black coffee (kafe shachor / botz) in glass cups.
- **06:30** — milking; feeding dogs and chickens; letting the flock out. Morning shepherd takes the
  flock east with a dog.
- **09:00** — bread + labneh + olives + vegetables breakfast; farm work: fencing, building, hauling
  water, weeding the vineyard, fixing the generator (again).
- **13:00** — flock back to shade through midday heat; lunch (pot of rice + lentils or pashtida);
  learning hour — some youth keep a Gemara/Tanakh session, or a visiting rabbi teaches.
- **16:00** — second grazing round toward sunset; visitors/volunteers arrive; supply run to the
  junction (hitchhiking — "טרמפים" — is the standard transport; standing at the tzomet with a hand out).
- **19:00** — maariv prayer; dinner around the fire; guitar; tea with sage; jackals start up.
- **Night** — guard shifts (שמירות) in rotation, 2–3 hours each, walking the perimeter with a flashlight
  and dog, checking the pen; volunteers from guarding organizations sometimes fill shifts.

Weekly rhythm — **Shabbat** is the anchor:
- Friday: frantic cleaning, cooking (chamin/cholent pot buried in coals or on a gas plate), showers,
  white shirts appear, generator timed or off, phones off for the observant.
- Shabbat: prayers, big meals, singing (zemirot), long naps, walking (no driving), Torah reading; the
  flock still must be watered/fed (permitted care of animals), but no building, no fires lit anew.
- Motzaei Shabbat (Saturday night): havdalah with candle and spices, then often the week's biggest
  **kumzitz (קומזיץ)** — bonfire, guitar (Carlebach niggunim, Breslov songs, Israeli folk), poyke,
  marshmallows, stories, until very late.
- Rosh Chodesh, holidays: Sukkot means building a sukkah of pallets and palm fronds; Passover means
  scouring the caravans; Lag BaOmer a giant bonfire; Tu BiShvat planting saplings.

Game translation: a 7-day cycle where day 7 halts construction/economy but regenerates "spirit,"
with kumzitz as a triggerable morale event on night 7's end.

## 4. Appearance & Clothing

- Large knitted kippa (כיפה גדולה) covering much of the head — white with pattern, or colorful; some
  wear it slightly askew. A few wear tembel hats or wide-brim sun hats over the kippa.
- **Peyot (פאות)** — long, often curled, tucked behind ears or flying loose; teenage wispy beards.
- **Tzitzit (ציצית)** worn out, strings swinging over the trousers — a defining silhouette in motion.
- Clothing: loose button shirts (often white, always dusty), plain t-shirts, worn work pants or shorts,
  big sweaters/fleece in winter, IDF-surplus dubon (parka) coats.
- **Sandals** (Shoresh/"Bible sandals") year-round, or work boots in mud season; often barefoot around
  the zula.
- Accessories: sling (קלע) tucked in belt, shepherd's stick (מקל), water bottle on rope, wool poncho,
  keffiyeh-style scarf against sun is NOT typical — a plain scarf or shirt over the head is.
- Girls/young women on family farms: long skirts, sandals, headscarf (mitpachat) if married; farms are
  often gender-separated among the youth, with family farms mixing families + boys' crews.
- Overall look: sun-tanned, lean, dusty, smiling; the aesthetic is deliberately simple and anti-polished.

## 5. Speech Style & Slang (30+ expressions)

Speech mixes army-adjacent Israeli slang, religious phrases, and Breslov-inflected warmth. Sentences
are short, warm, full of blessings. Collect for dialogue barks (all usable in Hebrew UI):

Address & warmth:
1. אחי (achi) — "bro," universal address.
2. אחוקי / אחינו (achuki / achinu) — affectionate "our brother."
3. כפרה עליך (kapara alecha) — "you're beloved," term of endearment.
4. נשמה (neshama) — "soul," addressing someone dear.
5. צדיק (tzadik) — "righteous one," friendly address: "מה קורה צדיק?"
6. מלך (melech) — "king," praise: "אתה מלך."
7. גיבור (gibor) — "hero/champ."
8. יא ורד (ya vered) — playful "you rose."

Faith phrases (constant, reflexive):
9. ברוך השם (baruch Hashem) — "thank God," answer to "how are you."
10. בעזרת השם / בעזרה (b'ezrat Hashem) — "God willing," attached to any plan.
11. אם ירצה השם (im yirtze Hashem) — same, more formal.
12. חסדי השם (chasdei Hashem) — "God's kindnesses," when something goes right.
13. הכל לטובה (hakol letova) — "it's all for the good" (Breslov), when something goes wrong.
14. אין ייאוש בעולם כלל (ein ye'ush ba'olam klal) — "there is no despair at all" (Rebbe Nachman).
15. השם יעזור (Hashem ya'azor) — "God will help."
16. בסייעתא דשמיא (b'siyata dishmaya) — "with Heaven's help."
17. תודה לאל (toda la'El) — "thanks to God."

General slang:
18. סבבה (sababa) — fine/cool.
19. אחלה (achla) — great.
20. וואלה (walla) — "really?/wow," all-purpose.
21. יאללה (yalla) — "let's go."
22. חבל על הזמן (chaval al hazman) — "amazing" (lit. waste of time).
23. פצצות (ptzatzot) — "bombs" = awesome.
24. על הפנים (al hapanim) — "terrible."
25. מה נסגר (ma nisgar) — "what's the deal?"
26. תכלס (tachles) — "bottom line / honestly."
27. סוף הדרך (sof haderech) — "end of the road" = incredible.
28. חזק וברוך (chazak u'varuch) — "strong and blessed," said after someone does something good / after aliyah to Torah.
29. תתחדש (titchadesh) — "enjoy the new thing."
30. בקטנה (biktana) — "no big deal."
31. שיהיה בהצלחה (sheyihye behatzlacha) — "good luck with it."
32. יישר כוח (yishar koach / shkoyach) — "well done."
33. עם ישראל חי (Am Yisrael chai) — sung/shouted at celebrations.
34. הגבעה (hagiv'a) — "the hill," how they refer to home: "עולים לגבעה" (going up to the hill).
35. זולה (zula) — the chill spot; also "לעשות זולה" = to hang out.
36. קומזיץ (kumzitz) — bonfire sing-along.
37. טרמפ (tremp) — hitchhike; "לתפוס טרמפ בצומת."
38. מסירות נפש (mesirut nefesh) — self-sacrifice/dedication, a core value word.
39. שומר (shomer) — guard; "אתה בשמירה הלילה" — "you're on guard tonight."
40. ההוא מהמנהל (hahu mehaminhal) — "that guy from the Administration" (civil administration inspector), said with a sigh.

Speech style notes for writers: sentences open with "תשמע אחי..." ("listen bro..."); good news gets
"ברוך השם"; plans always get "בעזרת השם"; disasters get "הכל לטובה" through gritted teeth; humor is
dry and self-deprecating about the broken generator and the stubborn donkey.

## 7. Landscape, Flora, Fauna (Samaria hills)

Terrain: rounded limestone hills 500–900 m, ancient agricultural **terraces (מדרגות)** of dry stone
walls on every slope, rocky outcrops, dry stream beds (wadis), caves and ancient cisterns, ruins
(chirbe) with old olive presses. Views carry far — on clear days from high hills you see the coastal
plain and even the sea; east, the Jordan Valley haze.

Seasons/light: winter — green velvet hills, mud, fog sitting in valleys, dramatic storms; spring —
carpets of wildflowers; summer — bone-dry gold-brown grass, heat shimmer, dust devils, hazy sharav
(heat wave) days; autumn — first rain turns everything soft, smell of wet dust.

Flora: olive (ancient gnarled + planted rows), fig (te'ena), grapevine, pomegranate, almond
(blossoms white/pink in Shvat = late winter), carob, Palestine oak (alon) and terebinth (ela)
scrub, sabra cactus hedges (tzabar — fruit picked with tongs in summer), za'atar (hyssop), sage
(marva/marmaria for tea), thyme, thistles (very yellow, very spiky by summer), red anemones
(kalaniot) and cyclamens (rakafot) in winter–spring, poppies, asphodel, squill (chatzav) blooming
white in late August — the folk sign that summer is ending.

Fauna:
- **Jackals (תנים)** — the night soundtrack; packs howl in eerie rising choruses; bold around trash and
  weak lambs.
- **Wolves (זאבים)** — rarer, but real predators of flocks; also famously chew drip-irrigation pipes.
- **Wild boars (חזירי בר)** — nocturnal crop wreckers: plow up vegetable patches, gorge on grapes and
  figs, fear dogs and noise.
- **Striped hyena (צבוע)** — rare, huge, scavenger; a legendary sighting.
- **Mountain gazelles (צבאים)** — graceful, bounding off at dawn; protected and beloved.
- **Rock hyrax (שפן סלע)** — colonies on boulder piles, whistle alarms, sunbathe.
- **Foxes, porcupines** (quills found as treasures), **hares**, mongoose.
- Birds: hoopoe (duchifat, national bird), buzzards and kestrels riding thermals, chukar partridges
  exploding from underfoot, owls at night, swifts in spring, ravens tormenting the dogs.
- Reptiles: agama lizards doing push-ups on rocks, glass-black snakes, the feared tzefa (viper) in
  summer woodpiles; scorpions under stones (shake your boots).
- Domestic cast: sheep, goats, guard dogs, herding dog, donkey, horse (some farms ride to herd),
  chickens, doves, barn cats, bees (a few hives).

## 8. Real Challenges → Game Events (non-violent framing)

- **Demolition order (צו הריסה)** — an inspector arrives, tapes a document to the caravan. Game
  mechanic per design constraints: timer to (a) dismantle the structure yourself for a materials
  refund, or (b) run a "muster supporters" mini-campaign (phone chain, buses of yeshiva students
  arrive, lawyer letter) to freeze the order. Either path is resolved without violence.
- **Livestock theft (גניבת מקנה)** — masked, faceless rustlers try to load sheep onto a truck at night;
  driven off by dogs, floodlights, noise, and defenders. Real farms rely on volunteer night guards
  precisely against theft; a "call HaShomer volunteers" action fits perfectly.
- **Predator night** — jackal pack or lone wolf probes the pen; boars hit the vineyard; solved with
  dogs, guard shifts, better fencing, noise.
- **Drought / water crisis** — the spring slows in late summer; jerrycan runs multiply; a water tanker
  delivery costs shekels; animals suffer if unwatered.
- **Generator failure / fuel shortage** — night without light and refrigeration; milk spoils; someone
  hitchhikes to town for a part.
- **Winter storm** — tin roofs peel, the tent flattens, mud strands the pickup, a caravan leaks;
  lambing in a cold snap needs heat lamps.
- **Heat wave (sharav) + brush fire** — dry-grass fire threatens the hay pile; firebreak plowing and
  beating with branches.
- **Lost flock / lost lamb** — a ewe wanders into a wadi at dusk; search party with flashlights before
  the jackals find her.
- **Lambing/veterinary events** — difficult birth at 03:00, orphan lamb to bottle-feed, vaccination day.
- **Snake bite / scorpion sting** — evacuation-by-tremp drama; first-aid kit upgrade matters.
- **Supply run gone long** — the hitchhiking trip to the junction takes hours; no rides in the rain.
- **Volunteers arrive** — a youth group or "national service" girls come for a workday: big temporary
  labor boost + morale, but they must be fed.
- **Visiting rabbi / parents visit** — spirit boost; also gentle comedy (mother inspecting the kitchen).
- **Rosh-Hashana of trees / holidays** — Sukkot building, Passover cleaning frenzy, Lag BaOmer bonfire.
- **Cheese sale day / market stand** — convert milk to shekels at the junction stand.
- **First rain (יורה)** — everyone runs out to stand in it; free spirit boost, plowing unlocked.

## 9. Values & Tone (for writing)

Core self-image: pioneers (chalutzim) reviving biblical shepherd life; simplicity as an ideal, not a
failure; "mesirut nefesh" (devotion) is the highest compliment; hospitality is absolute — any visitor
gets tea and a place by the fire; deep, casual faith (God is mentioned in every third sentence,
lightly); love of the land expressed physically — planting, building, walking barefoot; music and
laughter matter as much as work; strong anti-materialism (a cracked phone, if any; some avoid
smartphones entirely, using "kosher" button phones); reverence for elders and rabbis mixed with
teenage anarchic energy; humor: dry, biblical puns, endless jokes about the donkey, the generator,
and city people ("עירוניים") who can't handle the outhouse.
